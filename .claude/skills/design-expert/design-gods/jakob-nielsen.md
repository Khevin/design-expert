# Jakob Nielsen

Co-founded NNg and codified the ten usability heuristics in 1994. They still hold because they describe human cognition, not screens. The vocabulary every interface review tags failures by.

Tags: `#heuristics` `#usability` `#cognition` `#principles`

## Why they matter

Nielsen is the reason your design reviews are evidence-based instead of opinion-based. In 1994 he published ten usability heuristics — refined since but unchanged in essence — and inadvertently gave the field its grammar. Every numbered tag in a modern critique (`[Heuristic 4]`, `[Heuristic 9]`) is his. The heuristics survived three decades of platform shifts because they are not statements about screens; they are statements about how human working memory, attention, and tolerance for friction interact with any system that responds to input. Touch did not break them. Voice did not break them. The watch face does not break them. Whatever the next surface is, it will not break them either.

The second reason he matters is that he made real research cheap. Before Nielsen, "user testing" was a thirty-thousand-dollar lab study with one-way mirrors. He published the finding that five users uncover roughly 85% of the usability problems in a study, which sounds like a methodological footnote and is actually a manifesto: the small team has no excuse not to test. The 5-user rule, "discount usability engineering," and the Nielsen Norman Group itself — co-founded with Don Norman in 1998 — turned usability from a luxury into a hygiene practice. The reason your team can run a usability review on a Wednesday afternoon and have actionable findings by Friday is Jakob Nielsen.

The third reason is the citation library. NNg has been writing focused, evidence-backed articles for thirty years, and the corpus is now the most-cited body of work in interface design. When the design-expert skill cites `NNg, "Maintain Consistency and Adhere to Standards (Heuristic #4)"`, that article is the receipt — and the receipt is what separates a critique from a vibe. Nielsen's project was to make taste illegal in places where evidence existed, and to give designers the vocabulary to argue from evidence when stakeholders pushed back.

## Principles they brought

The ten heuristics are not commandments but failure modes. Each one names a specific way an interface can betray its user, and each is general enough to apply across mediums and specific enough to be testable. Beneath the heuristics sits a deeper claim: usability is not a category of design alongside aesthetics — it is the floor that aesthetics builds on. A beautiful interface that fails on heuristic 1 (system status) is a beautiful screenshot of a broken interface. The heuristics force every reviewer to walk the same checklist and tag failures with the same numbers, which is why a Nielsen-trained review from one designer reads coherently to any other.

The portable principles for interface work:

- **The 10 heuristics are a checklist for reviewing and a guard rail for building.** Walk them in order; surface only the ones that fail; cite the article.
- **Five users beats no users every time.** The 5-user rule means budget is no longer an excuse for shipping untested.
- **Behavior data trumps design intuition.** Eyetracking, time-on-task, error rate, drop-off — these are the only things that win arguments against a stakeholder with strong opinions.
- **Users do not read; they scan.** The F-shape reading pattern — discovered through eyetracking studies — should reshape every dense layout.
- **Response time has thresholds.** Under 100ms feels instant; under one second feels responsive; over ten seconds, give the user something else to do.
- **Severity ratings matter.** Not every usability issue is a release blocker. Tag findings as catastrophic, major, minor, or cosmetic so the team can triage instead of drown.
- **Citation is the discipline.** When you say "this fails heuristic 4," you are not stating a preference; you are tagging the failure to a body of evidence the team can audit.
