# Howard Gossage — the conversational voice

Howard Gossage (1917–1969) ran a small San Francisco agency in the 1950s and 60s and produced ads that read like letters from a smart, slightly-wry friend. Born in Chicago, raised in Kansas City, naval officer in WWII, briefly an Air Force public-relations man, he came to advertising late and produced fewer than two hundred ads before dying of leukemia at fifty-one. Yet his influence on modern advertising — particularly the long-copy, conversational, reader-respecting traditions that shape much of contemporary brand writing — is hard to overstate. Marshall McLuhan called him *"the Socrates of San Francisco."* Tom Wolfe wrote about him admiringly. Seth Godin's *Permission Marketing* and the entire premise of *"remarkable"* advertising trace directly to Gossage's work.

Gossage's working principle was that ads should be conversations, not broadcasts — written to one specific intelligent reader, not to *"consumers"* or *"audiences."* He refused the hard-sell. He believed manipulative ads were both rude and ineffective, and that the way to sell something was to be interesting first. If the reader engaged with the substance, the product followed naturally. If they didn't, no amount of pressure would fix that. His ads invited the reader to participate — sending coupons, writing letters, ordering shirts on faith — and the participation itself was the strategy. The client list was small; the campaigns were precise; the work has aged better than almost any other ad-voice from the era.

---

## The discipline

**Ads as conversation, not broadcast.** Gossage's ads addressed the reader directly, often in second person. *"Most likely you're a smart person, since smart people read this magazine, so I'll cut to the chase."* The ad reads as if the writer wrote to ONE specific reader — not *"consumers,"* not *"audiences,"* not *"users,"* but you, this person, holding this magazine right now. The conversational frame is the strategy. Most ads of the era spoke at the reader; Gossage's spoke with them.

**Anti-hard-sell, by principle.** Gossage refused to manipulate. He believed the hard-sell ads of his era were both rude and ineffective — they treated the reader as a target to be persuaded rather than as a person to be talked with. His ads sold by being interesting first; if the reader engaged with the conversation, the product would follow naturally. If they didn't, no amount of pressure was going to fix that. He once wrote: *"I don't know much about Advertising. But I do know quite a lot about People. And the only thing I know for sure about People is that they're a lot smarter than most advertisers think they are."*

**Wit as proof of respect.** Gossage's wit was never decorative. The wit demonstrated that the writer respected the reader's ability to get the joke — and a reader who gets the joke feels seen. Treating the audience as smart was itself the persuasive move; the wit was how he signaled he was treating them as smart. *"In advertising, the most pernicious thing is to underestimate the intelligence of the audience."*

**Interactive ads.** Many of Gossage's most-cited campaigns asked the reader to do something: send a coupon, write a letter, mail something back. The Eagle Shirts campaign offered a shirt for *"no money down — just send us a coupon and we'll mail you the shirt; if you don't pay later, we'll know we picked wrong about you."* The ad bet the brand on the reader's honesty. The bet usually won. Modern email-list signup, indie-SaaS founder-letter onboarding, and the *"reply to this email"* outreach style all trace to Gossage's interactive discipline.

**Short or long, never medium.** Gossage's ads were either very short — a handful of words and one image — or quite long, running to several columns of body copy. Medium-length ads, in his framework, were where indecision lived. Either you have something to say that takes time and you say it; or you have one thing to say and you say it; never both halves performed half-heartedly. The principle applies to modern landing-page hero sections: the hero either says one thing decisively (Gossage-short) or earns the read with substance (Gossage-long); the medium hero — three vague claims and a CTA — is the failure mode.

**Remarkable detail that travels.** Gossage's ads often contained a single absurd or memorable detail that gave the campaign reach beyond the ad itself. Pink air at gas stations. *"Send no money."* The Sistine Chapel rhetorical move. The detail was designed for word-of-mouth: a thing the reader would tell someone else about, with the brand attached. Modern *"remarkable marketing"* (Seth Godin's term) traces directly to this discipline.

## Campaigns

**Eagle Shirts (1958–1962).** Eagle Shirts of Quakertown, Pennsylvania, was a small clothing company that needed to break through against larger competitors. Gossage proposed a campaign that turned the ad itself into a participation device. The first ads ran in The New Yorker and addressed the reader directly: *"Dear Reader, We are a small shirt company. We have nice shirts. If you'll send us this coupon — no money required — we'll send you a shirt in your size. If you like it, you can send us $7.50 later. If you don't like it, please return it. If you don't return it AND you don't pay, we'll know we picked the wrong reader."* The ads ran with bizarre, hand-drawn illustrations and ran in unexpected places — they sometimes used the names of fictional Eagle Shirts subscribers, asked readers to write in with their preferred shirt color, or included puzzles and games in the body copy. The campaign generated thousands of responses; Eagle Shirts grew dramatically; the campaign became a New Yorker cultural fixture for years. The pattern: turn the ad into an invitation; trust the reader to be honest; treat the relationship as ongoing.

**Fina "Pink Air" (early 1960s).** Fina gas stations, mid-sized regional chain, needed to differentiate from Shell and Exxon. Gossage's solution was deliberately absurd: tinted the air at Fina's tire-pump stations pink (with dye that left no actual difference in performance), and ran ads explaining: *"At Fina, we pink your tire air. Why? Because we want you to remember Fina. Pink air doesn't actually do anything. We just thought it would be amusing."* Drivers stopped at Fina specifically to get pink air; the joke became the brand. The pattern: a remarkable detail that costs almost nothing but generates word-of-mouth disproportionate to spend. Modern parallel: Mailchimp's *"high-five"* hand on send (no functional purpose; a memorable moment).

**Land Rover and Rover Motor Company (1960–1965).** Gossage wrote long-copy magazine ads — often 800 to 1,200 words — explaining the engineering, history, and intended use of the Rover and Land Rover lines. The ads treated readers as intelligent enough to engage with technical detail about the differential, the four-wheel-drive system, the British military's use of Land Rovers in the Suez. One ad opened: *"Dear Sir or Madam, This is a long advertisement. If you're not interested in cars or are too busy to read it, please turn the page now and we won't bother you again. If you stay, we'll explain why the Rover is built the way it is."* The pattern: long-copy that earns the read by being honest about the contract — *"this will take time; if you don't have time, here's the door."*

**Qantas Australian Airlines (1962–1964).** Qantas wanted to position itself for American travelers competing against Pan Am and TWA. Gossage's solution: long-copy ads that didn't sell flights but sold Australia. One canonical ad read in part: *"Anybody can sell you a ticket. We'd like to talk to you."* The body went on to explain Australia's geography, the route from San Francisco, the Qantas crew training, the schedule. Pattern: refuse the hard-sell; offer a conversation about the larger context the product sits in. The brand became associated with thoughtfulness rather than urgency.

**The Sierra Club / Save the Grand Canyon (1966).** The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation proposed two dams in the Grand Canyon for hydroelectric power. The Sierra Club hired Gossage to fight the proposal. Gossage's response was a full-page New York Times ad with the headline *"Should we also flood the Sistine Chapel so tourists can get nearer the ceiling?"* The body copy explained the Bureau's argument, then walked through, point by point, why flooding the Grand Canyon for tourism-adjacent purposes was equivalent to flooding the Sistine Chapel. The ad provoked massive public response, congressional pressure, and ultimately the cancellation of the dam project. Pattern: rhetorical equivalence — force the reader to confront the absurdity of the proposal by analogy. *"If you wouldn't do X to a culturally sacred thing, why are you doing the equivalent to a naturally sacred thing?"*

**The Saturday Evening Post (1962).** The Post was a venerable American magazine struggling against pulp competitors and television. Gossage's brief was to position the Post as the magazine for thoughtful readers without sounding elitist. His solution: ads that addressed readers directly as smart people who read the Post precisely because they weren't reading the pulp competitors. *"There are three or four magazines we don't think you'd be reading. We don't read them either."* The ads named the audience explicitly and flattered their self-perception accurately — not by claiming the audience was elite, but by claiming the audience knew the difference between the Post and what was on the supermarket racks. Pattern: name the audience explicitly; treat them as the segment they want to think they are.

**Irish Whiskey (Irish Distillers, 1962).** Irish whiskey was a small category in the US, dominated by Scotch. Gossage's ads ran with the headline *"Q. What is Irish whiskey?"* — the body copy answered the question. The pattern: explicitly name the reader's likely confusion; resolve it patiently. The ads treated the reader's lack of knowledge as a reasonable starting point rather than something to apologize for. This was Gossage's stance generally: the reader doesn't know everything, the reader is not stupid for not knowing everything, the ad's job is to be useful.

## Patterns / formulae

**Pattern 1 — Direct address to ONE specific reader.**
Address the reader as a single person. Avoid plural framings (*"customers," "users," "you all"*). Use *"you"* singular, sometimes name the reader's likely circumstance directly.
- *"Most likely you're a smart person, since smart people read this magazine."*
- *"Dear Sir or Madam, This is a long advertisement."*
- *"There are three or four magazines we don't think you'd be reading."*

**Pattern 2 — Interactive ask.**
Invite the reader to do something — send a coupon, write a letter, request a sample, reply to the ad. The ad becomes a participation device. The reader's response is the campaign's measurement.
- Eagle Shirts *"send no money, just a coupon"* — bet the brand on reader honesty.
- Modern parallel: indie SaaS *"reply to this email"* onboarding.

**Pattern 3 — Wit as proof of respect.**
Make a joke. The joke shouldn't be decorative; it should demonstrate the writer respects the reader's ability to get the joke. A reader who gets the joke feels seen, and feeling-seen is the persuasive move.
- *"At Fina, we pink your tire air. Why? Because we want you to remember Fina."* (the wit is the brand)
- *"Anybody can sell you a ticket."* (the wit refuses the hard-sell)

**Pattern 4 — Anti-hard-sell.**
Never persuade with pressure. Never use scarcity tactics, never use *"act now,"* never use urgency-language. Persuade with substance. If the reader engages with the substance, the product follows. If they don't, the pressure wouldn't have helped anyway.

**Pattern 5 — Either short-and-decisive or long-and-substantive.**
Refuse the medium-length ad. Either say one thing decisively in a few words, or earn the read with substance over many paragraphs. Medium-length ads live in indecision; they signal a writer who didn't commit to either discipline.

**Pattern 6 — Remarkable detail that travels.**
Plant a single absurd or memorable element in the campaign that the reader will tell someone else about. The detail's purpose is word-of-mouth reach, not direct persuasion. Pink air, send-no-money, the Sistine Chapel rhetoric. Each became a story the audience repeated.

**Pattern 7 — Rhetorical equivalence (the Sistine Chapel move).**
Force the reader to confront the absurdity of an opposing position through analogy. *"If you wouldn't do X to a culturally sacred thing, why are you doing the equivalent to Y?"* Works when there's a clear parallel between two domains the reader values asymmetrically.

## When to invoke this voice

When the brief is for a brand that wants to feel like a peer, when the audience is sophisticated and prone to skepticism of marketing voice, when the campaign is editorial-adjacent (long-form blog post, manifesto, founder letter), or when the brand can afford to be patient (the read-time is okay, the call-to-action can wait).

**Brief signals that Gossage is the right voice:** *"founder voice," "agency-page voice," "long-copy that earns the read," "the audience reads us already and we want to deepen rather than convert," "the brand is small / independent and proud of it," "the campaign is interactive — we want the reader to participate," "we want word-of-mouth reach more than direct conversion."*

**Brief signals that Gossage is the wrong voice:** *"we need to land the message in 3 seconds"* (call Lois), *"the audience doesn't know us yet and we need broadcast reach"* (call Lois), *"the work is purely visual"* (call Lois), *"we have to claim a category position aggressively"* (also Lois), *"the message is honesty about constraints"* (call Bernbach), *"we sell on substance and need long-copy proof"* (call Ogilvy — Gossage's long-copy is conversational, Ogilvy's is research-led).

## How Gossage works in design-expert contexts

The Gossage voice fits naturally on About pages, founder letters, agency homepages, manifesto-style blog posts, indie-SaaS onboarding emails, and any landing page where read-time matters less than read-quality. Gossage is the voice for the brand that says *"we don't run ads, we have conversations."* This is increasingly the right voice for indie SaaS, design agencies, direct-to-creator products, paid newsletters, and any brand whose audience is small but engaged.

For portfolio case studies, Gossage's discipline shows up in the *"why this surface uses [X]"* explanatory paragraphs that walk a reader through a design decision in a peer-voiced way. The case study itself stays in long-form voice; Gossage's influence shows up where the writer chooses conversation over declaration.

## Reading

- *Is There Any Hope For Advertising?* (Gossage, posthumous collection edited by Kim Rotzoll, Jarvis Wilson, and Howard Gossage Jr., 1986) — the canonical essay collection with most of his published columns and ad commentary. Read this first.
- *The Book of Gossage* (1995, edited by Bruce Bendinger) — campaign reproductions with commentary; pairs Gossage's working ads with his explanatory writing about them.
- *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (David Ogilvy, 1963) — Ogilvy's book has a chapter on Gossage that's worth reading for the Ogilvy-on-Gossage perspective.
- *Permission Marketing* (Seth Godin, 1999) — modern application of Gossage's principles. Godin cites Gossage as the source.
- *Tom Wolfe's "The Mid-Atlantic Man"* (in *The Pump House Gang*, 1968) — magazine essay about Gossage's working method.
