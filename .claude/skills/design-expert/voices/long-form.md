# Long-form

Case studies, blog posts, magazine pieces, articles, journalism — the long-form prose voice for editorial work. The reader has chosen to engage and is committing real attention (eight minutes for a scanning case study, twenty minutes for a deep-read magazine piece). The voice has to earn that attention, not assume it.

The exemplars are Work&Co's client work (`work.co/clients/pga-tour`, `lyft`, `ikea`, `gatorade`, `apple`), Pentagram's project pages, NYT Magazine long-form, The Atlantic features, Aeon. The voice across all of them is recognizable: peer-to-peer, evidence-led, plain. Decidedly NOT marketing-page voice; this is journalist-meets-senior-designer voice.

---

## The Work&Co tone

The Work&Co case-study voice is the canonical reference for portfolio long-form. Eleven moves consistent across PGA Tour, Lyft, IKEA, Gatorade, Apple, and adjacent cases:

**1. Cold opens.** Drop the reader into the project without preamble. *"Lyft asked us to redesign the driver app."* No "in today's fast-paced world" preface. The first sentence states the project; the second states the constraint.

**2. Plain words, no marketing fluff.** Strike from the vocabulary: *"elevate," "streamline," "unleash," "next-gen," "transformative," "redefine," "bridging the gap," "cutting-edge," "innovative."* Replace with the specific verb that names what was done: *"rebuilt," "redesigned," "shipped," "documented," "tested."* Marketing words signal that the writer didn't trust the work; plain words let the work carry the weight.

**3. Active voice everywhere.** Every chapter h2, every body paragraph, every figure caption, every KPI label uses an active subject doing an active verb. Passive constructions read as evasion of authorship — case studies are claims of authorship and the voice should match. Rewrite *"X was Y'd by Z"* as *"Z Y'd X."*

**4. Short declarative sentences. Fragments where they punch.** *"The tech worked. The design didn't."* The Work&Co rhythm trades complex sentence structures for short beats the reader can absorb at scanning speed. The 8-minute reader does not have time for nested clauses.

**5. Decision-walks visible inline.** Don't just state outcomes; show the thinking. *"We considered jumping straight to redesigning screens. We picked the slower path because every fix would have been local without a library underneath."* Naming the trade-off makes the discipline visible to a peer-designer reader and a junior-designer reader simultaneously, without lecturing either.

**6. Functional stakeholder mentions; never individual names.** Refer to *"the engineering team," "the operations lead," "two designers reported to me," "the call center."* Do not name individuals — names are noise to readers who don't know them, and naming risks attributing credit unevenly. Roles are clearer and durable.

**7. Numbers inline, anchored to evidence.** *"The call center resolved 20% more support tickets per day after rollout."* Specific, measured, named. Avoid round numbers that smell fabricated (50%, 99.99%). Avoid metrics that aren't real outcomes.

**8. Short headings as claims, not categories.** Sections aren't *"Process / Approach / Outcome."* They're statements: *"A new system."* *"Built in 12 weeks."* *"Drivers earn more."* The heading IS the claim; the body proves it.

**9. Direct quotes, attributed simply.** Pull quotes are the voice of someone real saying something specific — usually the client lead. Attribution is single-line and functional (*"— [role] at [company]"*). Never a block-quote rhetorical aphorism floating in the layout.

**10. Captions are minimal.** Sometimes a 4–5 word phrase. Captions add information; they never describe what the image already shows. If the surrounding prose already explains the image, the caption is dropped.

**11. Closing punch.** End with a short rhythmic line the reader carries away. *"Sit down. Do the work. The rest follows."* Cite the actual project quote if one exists; otherwise let three short clauses do it. The closing is a payoff, not a summary.

## Magazine and journalism long-form — the canonical exemplars

The Work&Co tone fits portfolio case studies — work done for clients, reported by the doer. Magazine and journalism long-form is a bigger, older tradition, and a portfolio case study is closer in voice to a Work&Co piece than to a Talese profile, but the discipline of the long-form canon (New Yorker, Esquire, Atlantic, Scientific American) is what case-study voice grew out of and what it should aspire toward when ambition warrants it. Read the canonical pieces; load the disciplines.

The exemplars to study, in order of how often they reward re-reading:

**Gay Talese, "Frank Sinatra Has a Cold"** (Esquire, 1966). Around 15,000 words. Sinatra refused the interview; Talese profiled him by observing him for months. Mosaic structure built from forty-some short scenes. The piece is the founding document of New Journalism and remains the canonical profile-piece against which others are measured. <https://www.esquire.com/news-politics/a638/frank-sinatra-has-a-cold-gay-talese/>

**Kathryn Schulz, "The Really Big One"** (New Yorker, 2015). Pulitzer winner. About 7,000 words on the Cascadia subduction zone earthquake threat. Stakes are revealed slowly; each section escalates the danger; the reader is led from "this could happen" to "this WILL happen." Closes with directives the reader takes seriously because the 7,000 words have earned them. <https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2015/07/20/the-really-big-one>

**Scientific American, "Do Octopus Brains Work Like Human Brains?"** Long-form science writing for general readers. Translates complex evidence with patient escalation. Names specific scientists, specific experiments, specific octopuses. Embraces tentativeness — *"we don't yet know," "evidence suggests"* — without losing momentum. <https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/do-octopus-brains-work-like-humans-or-is-there-another-way-to-be-smart/>

**John McPhee, "The Search for Marvin Gardens"** (New Yorker, 1972). Structured around a single Monopoly game played by the writer; real-life Atlantic City reporting overlays the board. The reader experiences the conceit as both literal and metaphorical. McPhee's broader canon — including *Annals of the Former World* (1981–1998) — defines New Yorker structural ambition.

**Joan Didion, "Slouching Towards Bethlehem"** (Saturday Evening Post, 1967). Authorial voice detached but not cold; the writer is observer of a culture, not member of it. Scene-fragments build a sociological reading. No thesis stated; the thesis emerges. The standard for the personal-essay-as-cultural-analysis form.

**Ta-Nehisi Coates, "The Case for Reparations"** (Atlantic, 2014). Builds historical claim brick by brick. Single specific subject (Clyde Ross) anchors the abstraction. Voice is patient, almost pedagogical, but never lecturing. Conclusion lands as moral inevitability.

**David Foster Wallace, "Consider the Lobster"** (Gourmet, 2004). Lobster festival essay that becomes a meditation on animal suffering. Pivots register mid-piece; uses long footnotes that work like asides; the writer's mind IS the structure. The standard for the writer-as-thinker mode.

**Susan Orlean, "The American Man, Age 10"** (Esquire, 1992). Profile of one specific kid. The piece works because the specifics are unrepeatable and yet read as universal. The standard for profile-as-window: one subject framed precisely enough that they hold the universal claim.

Several broader contemporary writers belong on any long-form reading list: Janet Malcolm (*The Journalist and the Murderer*), Geoff Dyer (essays), John Jeremiah Sullivan, Jia Tolentino's New Yorker work, Patrick Radden Keefe (*Say Nothing*; the Sackler-family investigation), Lawrence Wright. Read in that direction when the canon above runs out.

For ongoing surveys of the form, the r/longform subreddit's *top-of-all-time* and *top-of-the-past-year* threads are reliable curation — not for the discussions themselves but for the linked articles, which point at the kind of pieces other long-form-readers find unforgettable. <https://www.reddit.com/r/longform/top/?sort=top&t=all> · <https://www.reddit.com/r/longform/top/>

## Patterns by exemplar — formulae you can reuse

The canonical pieces share recurring structural moves. Each pattern below names a formula you can apply when the writing context warrants long-form ambition.

**The Talese profile.** Subject often refuses interview; piece is built from observation. Voice is "the watcher in the room with you." Specific objects (Sinatra's cold, his glass, his suit) carry meaning; the writer never characterizes directly. Mosaic structure: many short scenes, each adding to a portrait. The reader concludes the subject's character from the accumulated specifics. *Use when:* the subject is unavailable, or the goal is to render character through environment rather than through the subject's own words.

**The McPhee structural conceit.** Single conceit (a board game, a season, a road trip, a geologic period) provides the spine. Real reporting fills the spine. The reader experiences the conceit as both literal and metaphorical. Conclusion delivers the metaphorical reading retroactively, after the literal has been built out. *Use when:* the subject is sprawling and needs an organizing object the reader can hold while you do the reporting.

**The Schulz disaster-piece.** Stakes are revealed slowly; each section escalates the danger. Specific scientists named, specific evidence cited. The reader is led from *"this could happen"* to *"this WILL happen"* through accumulated precision. Closes with directive action — *"if you live in the Pacific Northwest, do this"* — because the danger has been earned by the prose. *Use when:* the topic is urgent but unfamiliar; the reader needs to be brought from indifferent to invested.

**The Didion essay.** Authorial voice detached but not cold. Scene-fragments build a sociological reading. No thesis stated; the thesis emerges. Endings are often interrupted, refusing closure. *Use when:* the topic is cultural rather than factual, and the writer's tentativeness IS the position.

**The Coates argument-piece.** Builds historical claim brick by brick. Single specific subject anchors abstraction. Voice is patient, almost pedagogical, but never lecturing. Conclusion lands as moral inevitability rather than rhetorical force. *Use when:* the argument requires historical context the reader does not yet hold.

**The Scientific American science-piece.** Translates complex science for general readers. Names specific scientists, specific experiments, specific subjects. Embraces tentativeness without losing momentum. Structural escalation: *"here's what we thought"* → *"here's what changed"* → *"here's what's now possible."* *Use when:* the goal is to bring a non-expert reader to expert-adjacent understanding without lecturing.

**The Wallace mind-as-structure essay.** The writer's thinking IS the structure. Long footnotes work like asides. Register pivots happen mid-piece (a festive opening pivots to ethical reckoning). Self-aware but never coy. *Use when:* the writer is the right authority for the topic and the topic earns the writer's full attention.

**The Orlean profile-as-window.** Profile of one specific subject framed precisely enough that they hold a universal claim. Specifics are unrepeatable and yet read as universal. *Use when:* a single subject can stand in for a larger pattern without flattening the subject.

## Top 0.01% observed patterns

Across the canon above, a smaller set of patterns recurs and seems to mark the difference between *competent* long-form and *unforgettable* long-form. These are dispositions rather than rules. Internalize them; let them shape sentences without becoming a checklist you tick.

- **Lead with a specific moment, not an abstract claim.** *"On a Tuesday in October, Frank Sinatra was at the Beverly Wilshire bar with a cold he couldn't shake."* No "in today's world" preamble. Drop the reader into a place; let the place do the abstract work.
- **Show, never tell, at the sentence level.** Specifics earn the reader's trust; abstractions exhaust it. *"The redesign improved efficiency"* fails. *"The call center resolved 20% more support tickets per day"* succeeds. Apply at every sentence, not just at section openings.
- **The writer's voice is consistent but not loud.** The reader should know the writer is there but never feel performed-at. Talese's voice is rigorous observation; Schulz's is the science-explainer who knows when to step back; Coates's is patient pedagogy. None of them are performing.
- **Long sentences earn their length.** A sentence over thirty words must do real work; otherwise cut to two sentences. Long sentences are often a sign the writer didn't make a decision; cutting them is the discipline.
- **The piece is a designed object.** Structure carries meaning. Scenes, beats, returns, escalations are deliberate. Pieces without structural ambition feel like assembled paragraphs; pieces with structural ambition feel like designed objects. McPhee's Monopoly board, Schulz's escalating stakes, Coates's brick-by-brick chronology — all structural decisions before they were sentences.
- **Endings refuse the obvious thesis.** A great long-form ending lands somewhere unexpected but feels inevitable in retrospect. Resist the impulse to summarize; let the accumulated weight of the piece do the closing.
- **The ratio of specific-named-things to abstract-claims is high.** Talese's piece names dozens of specific objects, people, places. Schulz's names specific scientists, specific tectonic plates, specific past earthquakes. The specificity IS the authority.
- **Length earns itself.** A 7,000-word piece must justify its 7,000th word. Each paragraph should give the reader something. Pieces that pad lose the reader regardless of how good the opening was. The discipline that separates real long-form from inflated short-form is willingness to cut.

These are aspirational patterns for case studies and long-form blog posts that want to read like the canon. Most case studies will not need to operate at this level of ambition, and that is fine — the Work&Co tone above is the practical floor. Knowing the canon means knowing what's possible; when the writing context warrants the ambition, the patterns are available.

## Strunk and White as the floor

The Elements of Style applies to long-form with two caveats. Read the book; load the discipline. Then know where it bends.

**Where Strunk and White directly applies (no bending):**

- *"Omit needless words."* Long-form prose is full of cuttable adverbs and qualifying clauses. *"The team began to start working on…"* loses to *"The team started…"* loses to *"We started…"*. Cut.
- *"Use definite specific concrete language."* Specifics earn the reader's trust; abstractions exhaust it. *"The redesign improved efficiency"* fails. *"The call center resolved 20% more support tickets per day"* succeeds.
- *"Prefer the active voice."* Already covered above; Work&Co goes further than Strunk and White on this.
- *"Place yourself in the background."* Long-form case studies are about the work and the user, not about the designer's brilliance. The "I" should be functional — narrating choices — not biographical.
- *"Do not affect a breezy manner."* The temptation in modern long-form is to start every paragraph with *"So,"* or *"Look,"* or *"Here's the thing."* Resist. Breezy openers signal a writer who didn't trust the next sentence to carry weight on its own.

**Where Strunk and White bends:**

- *"Avoid foreign languages."* Modern editorial uses functional jargon when it's the right word. *"Component library," "design tokens," "rail-and-body grid"* are not English-as-foreign-language; they're technical English. Use them when they're load-bearing.
- *"Avoid the elaborate, the pretentious, the coy, and the cute."* Mostly right. But case studies and editorial pieces sometimes use a single moment of wit or warmth at the close to give the reader a payoff. *"Sit down. Do the work. The rest follows."* is mildly cute; it earns its place because the rest of the piece is plain.
- *"Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause."* Strunk and White's punctuation rules are sometimes too rigid for modern editorial rhythm. Em-dash and colon usage in long-form is more flexible than the book allows.
- *"Use orthodox spelling."* Editorial work occasionally uses proper-noun stylings (Pentagram's lowercase, IBM Carbon's all-caps) that violate orthodox capitalization but are required for accuracy.

The book is the discipline; the voice file is how the discipline gets applied. When in doubt, follow Strunk and White and let your editor argue. When you're confident, let the rhythm of the piece win.

## Title and subtitle patterns

**Case-study titles** mention the client by name. Capture both the experience claim and the system claim if both are real. Lead with active voice.

- *"We redesigned three apps for Spaces and built one shared system underneath."*
- *"We rebuilt Lyft's driver app — and the surface drivers spend their working hours inside."*
- *"PGA Tour. A new app, built in twelve weeks, before the season."*

**Subtitles** introduce the company in one sentence and name what we did in the second.

- *"Spaces runs contactless parking from New York to Texas to the West Coast. We redesigned its three apps — admin, operator, parker — and the design system underneath them."*

**Blog post titles** can run looser than case-study titles. Direct claim, often a clean two-beat structure. *"What we learned building three case studies in three months."* No clickbait.

## Caption discipline (three-beat rhythm)

Hero key-visual and chapter-pivot captions follow a three-beat structure: name the artifact, name the contrast, name the value or state.

*"Admin V2.0 in dark mode. The inherited screens had crammed five years of features into cluttered light-mode cards. This one breathes — one library, multi-region data, work that scans."*

For Before/After pairings, tag the "before" image with a small mono-uppercase chip. The "after" rarely needs an "After" tag — the prose around it does the work. Tagging only the inherited side preserves visual restraint.

---

## Drop "surface" jargon

Use "app" or "screen" or the actual product name. *"Surface"* is design-school vocabulary that reads as cold to non-designers and abstract to designers. *"App"* is direct and concrete.

## When to use this voice

When the writing is for a portfolio case study, a long-form blog post, a magazine-style company essay, an article, an editorial-adjacent piece. When the reader has actively chosen to engage. When the piece will be on the page for more than 60 seconds.

**When NOT to use this voice:** UX copy (use `voices/ux-copy.md`), landing-page heroes and ad campaigns (use `voices/marketing/`), founder letters that lean conversational rather than journalistic (consider `voices/marketing/howard-gossage.md` for a smaller, more intimate voice).
