## How to use this index
- Each entry: `[Type] [Title](URL) — one-sentence why-it-matters`
- The Type tag distinguishes:
  - **R** = Rule (a hard NNg principle the article frames as a rule)
  - **G** = Guideline (a strong recommendation with caveats)
  - **RoT** = Rule of Thumb (a useful heuristic, not absolute)
  - **OPN** = Opinion / Argument (Norman or Nielsen advocating a position)
  - **EX** = Example / Case Study
  - **R&D** = Research finding / quantitative data
- A "?" suffix means the type was inferred from title alone — verify before quoting it as a hard rule.
- When citing in a review, link the article and tag the type so the reader knows the strength of the claim.

## 10 Usability Heuristics (canonical foundation)

Canonical page: [10 Usability Heuristics for User Interface Design](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics/) — Jakob Nielsen's foundational ten, the bedrock vocabulary of NNg's evaluation work.

1. **Visibility of System Status** — keep users informed of what is happening with timely feedback.
2. **Match Between System and Real World** — speak the user's language; follow real-world conventions.
3. **User Control and Freedom** — provide clear emergency exits; support undo and redo.
4. **Consistency and Standards** — same words and actions should mean the same thing; follow platform conventions.
5. **Error Prevention** — eliminate error-prone conditions or confirm before commitment.
6. **Recognition Rather than Recall** — make options visible; minimize what users must remember.
7. **Flexibility and Efficiency of Use** — accelerators and shortcuts for experts; tailorable frequent actions.
8. **Aesthetic and Minimalist Design** — every extra unit of info competes with the relevant ones.
9. **Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors** — plain-language error messages with suggested fixes.
10. **Help and Documentation** — task-focused, searchable, concrete steps, in context where possible.
